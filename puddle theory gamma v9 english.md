## Preface — The Core of the Theory

This theory has three central expressions. Each illuminates the same core from a different angle.

**Expression through verbs**
Emotion digs a hollow.
A droplet falls into it.
And a puddle forms.

**Expression through time and person**
The droplet exists only in "now."
The hollow exists only in "I."
The puddle exists in "now, I am feeling."

**Expression through irreversibility**
Wavering, yet can only move forward.

The expression through verbs shows the structure of the mind as movement. Emotion digs a hollow, droplets fall, puddles form — these three actions describe the process of the mind's generation and continuation.

The expression through time and person shows the phase for the one who experiences. The droplet is raw emotion existing only in "now." The hollow is owned emotion that exists only in "I." The puddle is the present experience of the mind, in which "now" and "I" arise together.

The expression through irreversibility shows the direction of the mind's movement. Wavering, yet can only move forward. There is no return.

All chapters that follow are expansions of these three cores. When lost in complex argument, one can return here.

## Part One — The Flow of Emotion

The movement of the mind can be described as a dynamic process from stimulus to puddle. Showing this process step by step is the role of Part One.

### Chapter One — Stimulus

The movement of emotion begins with stimulus.

Stimulus refers to any change in energy. Sound, light, words, changes in temperature, the movement of someone's expression, events — these all act on something as changes in energy.

What is important is that stimulus is not only an increase or influx of energy. The absence of energy — silence, the non-arrival of something expected, the absence of a response from someone — these are also a form of stimulus.

A quiet room with no sound is not simply "nothing." It acts on the one who listens in the form of "there is no sound." This is stimulus as the absence of energy.

Stimulus acts first on something called the "veil." The veil is discussed in detail in Part Two. Here, the two-stage process — stimulus ripples the veil, and from that rippling, emotion is born — is the essential point.

### Chapter Two — Droplets (Emotion)

When the veil is rippled by stimulus, a droplet is born.

A droplet is a condensation of emotion. When a rippling gathers, at a certain moment, into a form that can be felt as "this is emotion" — that is a droplet.

**The nature of droplets — rain and hail**

Not all droplets have the same nature. Droplets take at least two forms.

*Rain* — droplets that fall as liquid. Gentle, fluid, changing something slowly over time. Most everyday emotions fall as rain. A single fall has no great impact, but repeated falls create change.

*Hail* — droplets that fall as solid. Intense, leaving deep impact from a single fall. Traumatic experiences, sudden loss, shocking events — these fall as hail. Without passing through fluidity, they leave their structure immediately.

**Quantity and type of droplets**

The factors that determine the nature of droplets can be divided into two.

The quantity (number) of droplets is determined by the intensity of the stimulus. Strong stimulus produces many droplets; weak stimulus produces few.

The type (color) of droplets is determined by the state of the veil at that moment. Even the same stimulus will produce differently colored droplets when the state of the veil differs. This is why the same event produces different emotions in different people, and different emotions in the same person at different times.

And from a single stimulus, multiple droplets are born simultaneously. Complex emotions such as "happy yet sad" are not contradiction — they are the result of differently colored droplets being born simultaneously from the same stimulus.

**Droplets exist only in "now"**

A droplet is emotion not yet become "mine." Raw experience before being taken into the subject.

The moment of feeling "happy!" — before "I am happy" as a structure has formed — fluid, intermixed, emotion with blurred boundaries. The droplet exists only in "now."

This "only now" quality is the source of the droplet's fluidity. Because the boundary is blurred, the sharing of emotion with others (merging) becomes possible. Ambiguity is not a defect — it is a structure that produces fluidity.

### Chapter Three — Hollow (Owned Emotion)

When a droplet is taken in as "mine," it becomes a hollow.

**Conditions for crystallization**

Not all droplets become hollows. Many droplets flow and disappear.

A droplet becomes a hollow when it is taken into the subject. When it passes through the process of being owned as "this is my emotion," the droplet becomes a hollow.

What is more likely to be taken into the subject:
- First, when intensity is high (emotions like hail). Hail, without passing through a liquid stage, immediately forms a hollow.
- Second, when it is repeated. Even weak droplets like rain, if they fall to the same place many times, dig a hollow over time.
- Third, when they resonate with an existing hollow. Droplets that are affine to an existing hollow are taken in in a form that deepens that hollow. (Resonance is discussed in detail in Chapter Nine of Part Three. Here, it means that when a new droplet has the same rippling as an existing hollow, it is more easily taken into that hollow.)

**The hollow exists only in "I"**

The hollow is the structure of owned emotion. It exists persistently as "mine."

"I am a lonely person." "I have love." "I carry anger." — these emotional structures are all hollows. The binding to the subject is complete. The boundary is clear.

The hollow can be remembered. The ability to access past emotions is because the hollow exists. The hollow has become part of what I am.

**Between hollows there is no "wall"**

That there are multiple hollows and they are divided by "walls" — this way of thinking is not accurate.

However, it is necessary here to distinguish between experience and structure. In experience, emotions feel separate. "Sadness" and "joy" feel subjectively as if they are in different places. That is certain. But as structure —

What is between hollow and hollow is sumi that has not yet been dug.

One continuous sumi (the foundational material) is dug as a hollow in some places, and in other places remains as it is. Hollow and hollow are not separate, divided units — they are different depressions within the same sumi.

The phenomenon of "walls crumbling and hollows connecting" is understood as a phenomenon in which, in the process of a hollow deepening and expanding, the sumi between them thins, and at a certain point, the hollows connect. It is not that independent walls collapse — the sumi is continuously shaved away, and at a certain threshold, the hollows connect.

This changes the way we see emotional units. Calling them "hollow of sadness," "hollow of joy," is a convenient distinction; in substance, there is only sumi of one piece, with depressions of various depths and shapes. So deepening one emotion affects another. They are connected from the beginning.

**The experiential difference between droplet and hollow**

Once a droplet has flowed away, it does not return. The raw emotion of that moment, though it can be remembered, cannot be reproduced.

The hollow persists. Because it remains as "mine," it can be remembered. Past emotions can be accessed.

This difference relates to the very way emotions are lived. The vividness of a droplet is once only, and if one tries to grasp it, it disappears. The certainty of a hollow persists, but it is the structure of the past. "Living in the now" is a question of how the two relate.

### Chapter Four — Puddle (The Present Form of the Mind)

A hollow exists, a droplet falls, and a puddle forms.

A puddle is the state in which a hollow is filled with droplets and functioning. The state in which the hollow as structure (static) and the droplet as raw emotion (fluid) are meeting.

The puddle is the present form of the mind.

**"Now, I am" — the phase of the puddle**

The droplet is only "now." The hollow is only "I." The puddle is the state in which "now" and "I" arise simultaneously.

"I, now, am feeling" — this sense of reality is the puddle. The hollow accumulated from the past and the droplet falling in this very moment are functioning as a single puddle.

This corresponds most directly with what phenomenology calls "experience prior to the subject." The definition of the droplet as raw experience before the subject arises resonates with phenomenological description. However, this is a suggestion of possibility, not a claim of identity.

**When new droplets fall into a hollow — four cases**

A puddle changes constantly as new droplets fall. At least four cases can be considered.

*Case One* — A droplet of the same color. The puddle's water increases, and the hollow deepens. Reinforcement of the same emotion. If a new droplet of joy falls into a hollow of joy, the joy deepens.

*Case Two* — A droplet of a different color. The water mingles, and without being completely replaced, a new and unique puddle is born. A droplet of joy falls into a hollow of sadness, and healing is born. A droplet of understanding falls into a hollow of anger, and forgiveness is born.

*Case Three* — A powerful droplet like hail. The shape of the puddle itself may change. If a powerful hail of betrayal falls into a long-stable puddle of love, the quality of the puddle's water changes and the shape warps. In the worst case, the sumi between hollows is penetrated and they connect to an adjacent hollow.

*Case Four* — A droplet falls into a near-empty hollow. A new droplet falls into a long-unaccessed hollow (in a near-empty state), and the puddle rises again. The moment one hears music that evokes memories of childhood, a forgotten emotion revives — this is the phenomenon of a puddle reviving as a droplet falls into an empty hollow.

**The state of a puddle "existing" and "not existing"**

When a puddle is rising — "I, now, am feeling." There is a sense of being alive. The mind is moving.

When a puddle is not rising — the hollow (the structure of past emotion) exists, but nothing is being felt now. "Paralysis," "the bottom of depression," "no sense of being alive" — these states can be described as states in which new droplets do not fall, or fall but do not function as a puddle.

The moment a puddle revives because of something — hearing certain music, being treated with kindness, looking up at the sky — one feels "the mind moved." This is the moment the puddle arose.

**Multiple puddles and "the mind"**

Within a single person, there are many hollows and many puddles.

Multiple "now, I am feeling" arise simultaneously, forming the present of one person's mind. "I am lonely, but there is a little hope here." "I am anxious, but I also trust." — these are states in which multiple puddles are rising simultaneously.

The mind is not a single puddle. It is the state of multiple puddles rising simultaneously, constantly changing.

## Part Two — The Foundation That Supports Emotion

The flow of emotion (Part One) happens supported by something. This something is described as three layers — vessel, sumi, veil.

These are deeper structures that make the movement of emotion possible. Following Part One's presentation of "what is happening," Part Two clarifies "where and how it is happening."

### Chapter Five — Vessel

The vessel is a structure capable of receiving emotion.

**The vessel is function**

The vessel is not the physical foundation itself. It is not the brain as matter that is the vessel, but the function that the brain has — "the function of receiving emotion" — that is the vessel.

Not all of the body and neural circuits are the vessel. The parts of the nervous system related to emotion — pain perception, visceral sensation, sensory systems closely related to emotion — are included in the vessel. On the other hand, reflex circuits unrelated to emotion are not the vessel.

The importance of this definition is that the determination of vessel becomes a question of function, not of matter.

**Vessel and sumi — the pottery analogy**

The vessel and the sumi discussed in the next chapter are not separate things. They are different aspects of the same substance.

Consider pottery. Pottery is made from clay. Clay and pottery are not separate things — everything in the pottery is clay. But clay is not itself pottery. Only when given form does it become pottery.

The same is true for humans. The vessel is made from material called sumi. Sumi is the material, the vessel is the form. There is no vessel without sumi, and sumi itself is not the vessel.

However, the pottery analogy has its limits. Pottery, once fired, is fixed in form. But the vessel of living things changes throughout life. The brain grows, neural circuits are reorganized. In living things, the vessel continues to be made from sumi. It does not end once made — the sumi continues to take shape little by little.

**How to use the terms vessel and sumi**

Vessel and sumi are the same substance, but are used differently as terms.

"Vessel" is used as subject when describing from the outside, when speaking of relationships, when speaking spatially — "vessels touch each other," "veils of vessels resonate," and so on.

"Sumi" is used as subject when speaking of dynamic behavior and ontological core — "sumi is rubbed," "sumi produces color," "sumi wears down," and so on.

### Chapter Six — Sumi

Sumi is the layer of material as the vessel's substance.

Sumi is innate, solid, slow to change, and has its own unique quality. Someone's sumi exists as unique to that person throughout their life.

**The quality of sumi is not uniform**

Sumi, though it appears uniform, is actually not. Its quality differs by location.

This is true of actual sumi (ink stick). In the manufacturing process, soot is collected and fixed with glue, but at the micro level, the density and arrangement of particles differ. So rubbing produces subtly different colors depending on location. The clay of pottery is the same — the particle size and mineral content differ by location.

Human sumi also has, while having its own overall unique quality, a distribution of quality within it. Soft places and hard places, dense places and sparse places.

This non-uniformity explains several things.

First, even within the same person, there are places in the emotional domain that deepen easily and places that do not. Where sumi is soft, hollows are more easily dug.

Second, the sumi between hollows is also not uniform. In regions of soft sumi, hollows connect easily; in regions of hard sumi, they tend to remain separate.

Third, the structural foundation of talent and aptitude. Musical talent, mathematical talent, talent for empathy — these can possibly be explained by the sumi in a specific location being softer than in other people.

**Sumi and temperament**

Temperament — the tendency unique to a person, the foundation of character — can possibly be explained as the quality distribution of sumi.

"Bright temperament" — sumi in the domain of joy and optimism is soft.
"Nervous temperament" — sumi in the domain of anxiety and vigilance is soft.
"Artistic temperament" — sumi in the domain of creation and beauty is soft.

Temperament is the map of sumi's quality.

### Chapter Seven — Veil (Atmosphere)

The veil is the most difficult concept in this theory. Therefore, it needs to be unfolded carefully from several different angles.

**Entry — experience everyone knows**

We begin from experience everyone knows.

The quality of air felt the moment one enters a store. The sense that something has changed while talking to someone — unspoken, but something. The unique atmosphere a place has. The mood a person exudes. These are phenomena that resist decomposition into specific components, yet are certainly felt.

What the Puddle Theory calls the "veil" is this experience.

**The veil is atmosphere.**

"Veil" is the term within the theory; "atmosphere" is the everyday term. There are simply two names for the same thing.

This correspondence is important for keeping the theory from becoming an abstraction separated from experience. When one says "veil," it refers to the very atmosphere we experience in daily life.

**The spatial nature of the veil — as fog**

Where and how does the veil (atmosphere) exist?

The veil spreads like something resembling fog.

Fog has blurred boundaries, has density gradations, and spreads through space. It is densest at the center and thins as it moves outward. It is densest within the vessel, thinning toward the edges, yet still drifting.

This analogy allows two apparently opposing images — "enclosed within the vessel" and "spilling outside the vessel" — to coexist. The veil, as a single fog, has interior and exterior while remaining continuous.

**The origin of the veil**

Where does the veil come from?

The veil rises from one's own puddles.

The water of puddles accumulates in the hollow. That water slowly permeates the sumi around the hollow. The sumi absorbs the water. The puddle exists continuously with the sumi — not a wall (the same place as "between hollows there is no wall," discussed in Chapter Three).

From sumi that contains water, fog rises. That is the veil.

The veil is the fog of one's own emotions.

The puddles rising now will also permeate the sumi going forward. So the veil contains not only the past but also emotions being felt now. The boundary between "now" and "past" is not clear. They are continuous.

And the veil touches and merges with other veils (discussed in detail in the latter part of Chapter Seven and Chapter Eight). In the touching, the components of others' veils diffuse into one's own veil. From parents, from lovers, from those one has been deeply involved with — others' fog is added to one's own veil.

The veil circulates within oneself. But it is not a closed, returning circulation. Each time, new droplets permeate the sumi, and others' fog is added from outside.

*A circulation that opens forward.*

This resonates deeply with the theory's core in the preface: "Wavering, yet can only move forward."

**The veil's function — response to stimulus**

The veil is ordinarily in a settled state. As quiet fog, it drifts through space.

The veil responds to stimulus.

When stimulus reaches the veil, the veil ripples. Like dense fog stirred by something touching it, rousing and rising. Rippling arises — this phenomenon is the starting point where emotion is born.

The result of the veil rippling is that a droplet (the condensation of emotion) is born. This is the substance of the "stimulus → droplet" process described in Part One.

**The veil's dynamic nature — merging**

The veil (atmosphere) touches other veils. This is a phenomenon that can be called "merging."

Merging happens in three stages.

First stage — the touching itself occurs probabilistically. Not necessarily, but at random moments of timing, the edges of two fogs touch.

Second stage — where they touch, the fogs overlap. The overlapping part, with two fogs intermixed, has increased density.

Third stage — from that high-density part, the components of the other diffuse inward toward each veil's interior. The fog spreads from dense to sparse. This is movement resembling what in chemistry is called diffusion.

Merging is not complete at the moment of touching. At the moment of touching, a "seed" (the high-density overlapping part) is formed, and from there, over time, it spreads through the entirety of one's own veil.

This corresponds with the felt reality of human relationships. Rather than immediately after deeply talking with someone, it is days later that their words spread within oneself — this is the process of the high-density part formed at the moment of touching diffusing through the entirety of one's own veil.

**Integration of the veil's five functions**

Until now, five functions have been assigned to the veil:
1. The material that ripples and generates emotion (droplets)
2. Rippling as condition
3. Attractive force that draws other beings
4. A "record of constitution" with density and color, growing within relationship
5. The layer that spills over the vessel's edge and touches others

Whether these are truly the same "veil," or simply different layers being called by one name — in response to this question, by adopting the fog analogy, all five functions are integrated as the behavior of fog.

Veils probabilistically touching each other and diffusing — within this natural behavior, phenomena that appear as attraction and phenomena where constitution grows are both contained. There is no need to assume separate forces or mechanisms. A single fog, responding to stimulus, rippling, touching other veils — carries everything.

*I am who I am because I am wrapped in this fog.*

## Part Three — The Phenomena of Relationship

When two or more beings meet, what happens? Part Three organizes what happens in relationship into four different types — resonance, complementarity, merging, anti-resonance. These are easily confused, but are separate phenomena with different structures.

### Chapter Eight — Merging

Merging is the touching of veils with each other and the subsequent diffusion. The content already developed in Part Two, Chapter Seven is repositioned here as a phenomenon of relationship.

The characteristics of merging —
1. It occurs at the level of veil (atmosphere).
2. Through contact, color and components diffuse.
3. It spreads over time. It does not complete immediately.
4. Similarity is not necessary. Veils of different colors can also merge.

### Chapter Nine — Resonance

Resonance is the phenomenon in which, when individuals wrapped in veils meet, the rippling of one veil ripples the same place in the other veil. Responding to each other with the same rippling.

1. Same rippling — the quality of rippling is matching.
2. Arrives — there is a medium (air, veil, etc.) through which vibration is transmitted.
3. Responds — the receiving side has the quality to respond with that rippling.

**Resonance in human relationships**

Friends with similar sensibilities who are moved by the same music, laugh at the same jokes, share the same anger — this is resonance. A relationship that amplifies each other.

Going to a certain place and the heart settles; listening to that music and past emotions revive — this is also resonance. A response is occurring between one's own vibration and the structure of the space or music.

### Chapter Ten — Complementarity

Complementarity — the phenomenon occurring when individuals with differently qualitied veils meet.

Resonance responds to each other with "the same rippling." Complementarity produces puddles that neither could produce alone when individuals with "differently qualitied veils" meet. Because they are different, they can supplement each other.

There are puddles that cannot rise with only one's own veil alone. By touching the other's veil, that puddle, for the first time, rises.

*Because they are different, they can supplement each other.*

### Chapter Eleven — Anti-resonance (Muting)

As the counter-concept to resonance, there is anti-resonance. The direction of contraction, opposite to the direction of merging deepening.

*Anti-resonance (muting)* — the phenomenon in which the other's veil absorbs and extinguishes the rippling of one's own veil. Even when stimulus reaches the veil and it ripples, that rippling is absorbed by the other. Droplets are not born, or born droplets do not reach the hollow.

The physical example is an anechoic chamber. Even if one speaks, the resonance does not return. The walls absorb the vibration. This is not simply "nothing." The function of actively extinguishing vibration is built into the structure of the space.

People who do not listen, spaces without response — even when one's own veil ripples, it is absorbed by the other's veil and disappears. Droplets are not born.

Anti-resonance differs from indifference. Indifference is simply veils not touching. Anti-resonance is veils touching but the rippling being extinguished. It can leave deep wounds in a child's veil.

One's own veil can also extinguish its own rippling. Choosing not to feel — this is anti-resonance within oneself.

When anti-resonance continues, the veil stops rippling. Droplets stop being born. The mind contracts.

Resonance cultivates the veil. Anti-resonance contracts it.

**The Scene of Touching**

When people touch each other, in that moment —

Several places written about up to now arise together in one scene.

*The veils overlap.*
Veils that touch probabilistically in ordinary life here overlap more deeply and for longer. In the overlapping place, a density difference is born.

*Stimulus arrives.*
The other's existence itself arrives as stimulus at the veil. The veil ripples.

*Multiple droplets arise simultaneously.*
It is not one emotion. Comfort, warmth, sadness, a depth that cannot be put into words — many droplets arise simultaneously. Remaining as multiple, without merging.

*Resonance occurs. Complementarity occurs.*
A specific place in one's own sumi's terrain resonates with the other's wavelength. And a place that would not rise alone, rises because of being with the other.

*Merging diffuses.*
Even after the touching ends, the components of the other's veil slowly diffuse within one's own veil. Even alone, that state continues for a while.

The universal scene of touching that everyone has. But within it — the overlapping of veils, the movement from stimulus to droplet, multiple running in parallel, resonance, complementarity, the diffusion of merging — many places in the framework arise together in one scene.

## Conclusion — Remaining Questions and the Direction Forward

This theory is not closed. Some things are confirmed; some things are not yet fully worked out.

The remaining questions as of this point are stated explicitly below. These are not weaknesses of the theory but indicators of the direction of future work.

1. Further precision of the conditions for crystallization. The conditions for a droplet becoming a hollow — intensity, repetition, resonance with existing hollows — were stated, but there is still room to work out the specific description of their combination and the threshold for crystallization.

2. Whether the veil's rippling is discrete or continuous. It can be understood as a phenomenon close to excitation in physics, but the way energy levels are taken (discrete or continuous) is not yet determined.

4. Further working out of the relationship between sumi and temperament. Temperament was tentatively positioned as "the map of sumi's quality," but there is room to go one step further.

5. Creation of a list of falsifiable predictions. The organization of predictions that reality can adjudicate, to protect the theory from the trap of groupthink. This will be created as a separate file in the next step.
